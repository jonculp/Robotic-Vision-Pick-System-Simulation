How it works — key concepts
Overhead vision · stereo depth + scene understanding
steps 1–6  (frame capture, YOLOv8 detect, depth+cloud, pose ICP, grasp lookup, IK solve)
runs once per cycle before arm moves
steps 1–3
SAD stereo matching — Sum of Absolute Differences: for each pixel in the left image, the matcher slides a block window across the right image and finds the horizontal offset (disparity) that minimises the difference. STEP=1 (every pixel), HALF=4 (9×9 block), MAX_DISP=64. IR dots provide high-contrast anchors that prevent void regions on uniform surfaces.
step 3
Depth formula Z = f·B/d — Converts disparity to metric depth. f = focal length in pixels, B = baseline in meters, d = disparity in pixels. A disparity of 82px with f=246px and B=1.0m gives Z ≈ 3m.
epipolar panel
Epipolar constraint — For a rectified stereo pair (both cameras aligned to the same horizontal baseline and looking in the same direction), any point visible in the left image must appear somewhere on a single horizontal line — the epipolar line — in the right image. Because this system is rectified, all epipolar lines are perfectly horizontal scanlines, which is why the SAD matcher only searches left/right and never up/down. The panel shows the left image probe point (green crosshair) and the corresponding orange epipolar line in the right image. The green dot is where SAD found the actual match; the bracket shows the disparity d. The dashed blue vertical marks where the match would fall if the object were at infinite distance (d = 0). Inject a fault to see the match disappear — depth becomes undefined when SAD confidence drops below threshold.
disparity GT
Ground truth disparity — A GPU depth pass from stereoL's exact position gives per-pixel true Z. Disparity is derived as d = f·B/Z. Auto-ranged so the small depth difference between parts and platform spreads across the full turbo colormap. Compare directly with the computed disparity panel to see where SAD loses information.
adaptive IR
Adaptive IR dot seeding — After each pick cycle, the void mask (pixels where disparity = 0) is dilated 8px, unprojected to world space, and filtered through a Poisson disk pass. New supplemental IR dots are seeded in those regions and stamped at slightly higher intensity on the next cycle. Toggle to IR or Combined mode to see the dots flash cyan when new ones are added.
learned matching
U-Net learned stereo — Production systems replace SAD with a trained encoder-decoder network. The training simulation shows convergence: voids fill in, noisy matches sharpen, accuracy asymptotes at ~85% (real network ceiling due to textureless regions and occlusion boundaries). Each epoch blends SAD output toward GT disparity.
steps 1–2
YOLOv8 object detection — A single-shot neural network runs on the left camera image and outputs a bounding box, class label (Box / Cyl / Sphere), and confidence score for every visible part. This is the entry point of the pipeline — it answers "what is on the platform and roughly where?" before anything else can run. The centroid pixel of the winning detection is projected into the disparity map to get a seed depth, which seeds the ICP pose estimator. Detection fires once during the scan phase and is not updated again during the pick — if a part moves after the scan, the system does not know.
step 3
Point cloud — Every pixel that has a valid disparity value is unprojected from 2D image space into 3D world coordinates using Z = f·B/d, then x and y are recovered from the pixel position and focal length. The result is a set of (x, y, z) points in metres — one per valid pixel — that describes the 3D surface geometry of the scene. In this pipeline it feeds two things: (1) it provides the seed 3D position for ICP pose estimation, and (2) the median depth sampled from the cloud at the part centroid refines the arm waypoint heights so the approach and pick positions match the actual measured part height rather than relying on assumed geometry.
step 4
Pose ICP (Iterative Closest Point) — Detection gives a 2D bounding box; the point cloud gives an approximate 3D position. Neither gives orientation. ICP takes the point cloud of the detected part and iteratively aligns it against a known 3D template of that part type. At each iteration it finds the closest template point for each cloud point, computes the rigid transform (rotation + translation) that minimises the total distance, and applies it. After convergence you have the full 6DOF pose — position and orientation — which determines the grasp angle. ICP is a local optimiser: it needs a good starting position (from detection + depth) to converge to the correct pose rather than a local minimum.

steps 5–6  (grasp lookup, IK solve)
computed from depth data before arm moves
steps 5–6
Inverse kinematics (IK) — Given a target XYZ position for the end effector (suction cup), IK works backward through the arm's joint geometry to compute the joint angles needed to reach it. This arm has two joints: q1 at the shoulder and q2 at the elbow. The solver outputs these two angles — not a position — and the motor controller drives each joint to its target angle. This sim uses a closed-form analytic solver for a 2-DOF planar arm. Waypoint heights are optionally refined from stereo disparity at the part centroid.
vision gating
Open-loop execution — Overhead stereo is used pre-motion only: scan the scene, plan the grasp, then execute open-loop. Once the arm enters the camera FOV (approach through lift), vision panels dim and recompute is paused. The arm cannot occlude what it already knows — the pick pose is committed before motion starts.
Execution · closed-loop arm motion with wrist cam and F/T
steps 7–16  (arm approach, arm descend, wrist verify, vacuum pick, vacuum confirm, lift, transit, place descend, release, return home)
wrist cam and F/T sensor close the loop during motion
steps 8–9 · wrist cam
Wrist camera — 90° FOV, straight down — Mounted rigidly to the same tool body as the suction cup, looking straight down. Wide FOV (90°) captures the full gripper footprint plus surrounding context at 5–20cm range. Always-on in monitoring mode; overlays activate during descent and pick. The camera is on its own render layer so its own housing never occludes the image.
step 8 · wrist verify
Why wrist cam is mono, not stereo — At 5–15cm from the part during descent, a stereo baseline wide enough to give useful depth would be wider than the gripper itself. Wrist cameras are monocular. Depth at that range comes from three other sources instead: (1) known part geometry — if YOLOv8 classified the part and you have a CAD model, apparent pixel size gives Z; (2) structured light from the gripper — some grippers have a tiny built-in IR projector; (3) force/torque feedback — the arm senses contact and stops descending.
step 8 · wrist verify
What the wrist cam is for — Not for recomputing depth — the overhead stereo already gave you Z. The wrist cam's job is grasp verification: confirming the part is still present and centred in the gripper FOV before vacuum engages. It catches cases where a part has shifted since the overhead scan, or where a neighbouring part is in the way. Activates during Arm descend and Wrist verify states.
step 8 · rim fit
Rim fit — The suction cup rim is a rigid circle of known radius (86mm). Its 3D position is always known from forward kinematics, so it can be projected into the wrist cam frame as an ellipse. The shape of that ellipse encodes the camera-to-cup pose — if the camera were perfectly coaxial the ellipse would be a circle. Fitting it gives a continuous self-calibration reference without any separate fiducial marker.
step 8 · offset vector
Part centroid and offset vector — During descent the part's 3D centroid (from YOLOv8 + overhead stereo depth) is projected into the wrist cam frame as an orange dot. A vector is drawn from the rim ellipse centroid to the part centroid. The length of that vector is the XY grasp error in millimetres — green if within tolerance, amber if the part has drifted since the overhead scan. In a real system this vector drives a correction move before vacuum engages.
steps 10–11 · F/T
F/T readout strip — The bottom strip of the wrist cam panel shows the force/torque sensor Fz reading (axial force along the tool Z axis) as a bar graph and numeric value. Green = contact confirmed, amber = slip detected. This is the primary signal the controller uses to confirm a successful pick — not vision. If Fz drops during transit, the part has been lost and the cycle aborts.
steps 10–11 · F/T
Force/torque sensor placement — The amber puck visible between the wrist joint and the suction cup stem is the F/T sensor. Placement is critical: it must sit in the load path between the robot flange and the tool, so it measures every force the tool experiences — touchdown contact, vacuum hold force, and lateral slip loads during transit. Mounting it on the camera bracket (outside the load path) would measure nothing useful. In this sim Fz is computed from part weight and contact state; in hardware it is a 6-axis strain gauge reading at ~1kHz.
System · calibration health
affects all 16 pipeline steps
calibration
Reprojection error — Measures stereo calibration quality: how many pixels a known 3D point projects away from its expected image position. Below 0.5px is good; above 0.5px indicates calibration drift and degrades depth accuracy. Use Inject fault to simulate a calibration failure and watch all vision panels degrade.
calibration · not simulated
Hand-eye calibration — Determines the rigid transform between the robot's tool-center-point (TCP) and the camera frame. Without this, depth measurements in camera coordinates can't be converted into robot joint commands. Performed once at setup by moving the arm to a set of known poses while a calibration target is in view, then solving for the unknown transform. Errors here shift every pick target by a fixed offset — the arm is consistently wrong in the same direction.
calibration · not simulated
Gripper and TCP calibration — The tool-center-point must be registered to the physical tip of the suction cup (or finger tips for jaw grippers). If the cup is replaced or bent, this offset changes. Separately, the wrist camera's pose relative to the cup rim must be measured so the rim-fit projection stays accurate. In practice these are measured with a touch probe or with structured light against a flat surface.
calibration · not simulated
Recalibration triggers — Stereo calibration drifts over time as cameras shift thermally or after physical disturbance. Production systems watch reprojection error continuously and flag a recalibration request when it exceeds a threshold. Other triggers: a pick miss rate spike (arm is missing targets), a force/torque anomaly pattern (contacts are off-center), or a scheduled maintenance window. Recalibration replays the original calibration routine without stopping the line if a parallel workcell is available.
