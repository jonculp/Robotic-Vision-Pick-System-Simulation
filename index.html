Model architecture
▌ Encoder (left, blue)
Each block halves the spatial resolution (320→160→80→40px) while doubling the number of feature channels (64→128→256). This forces the network to extract progressively more abstract features — edges become textures, textures become shapes.
▌ Bottleneck (center, bright blue)
The smallest block — 512 channels at the lowest resolution. This is where the network's full understanding of the scene is encoded. Every pixel's disparity must be derivable from this compact representation.
▌ Decoder (right, green)
Mirrors the encoder in reverse — each block upsamples back toward full resolution, reducing channels. Skip connections preserve fine edge detail that would otherwise blur out during the compression-expansion cycle.
▌ Skip connections (dashed lines)
Direct shortcuts that copy encoder feature maps across to the matching decoder layer at the same resolution. They preserve fine edge detail (object outlines, surface texture boundaries) that would otherwise blur out.
▌ Why ~85% accuracy ceiling?
No stereo algorithm — learned or classical — can match pixels it cannot see. Occlusion boundaries, featureless surfaces, and specular highlights all produce invalid or ambiguous matches.
▌ What this sim is actually doing
Each epoch linearly blends the SAD disparity output toward the ground truth (GT) disparity from the GPU depth pass — approximating how a real network would converge.

How it works — key concepts
camera panel toggle
RGB / IR / Combined modes — Each camera panel has a cycle button. RGB shows the raw Three.js render (color, no dots). IR shows only the structured-light dot pattern on a black field — exactly what a real IR camera sensor sees. Combined overlays the IR dots on the RGB image at ~85% opacity, showing how the projector illuminates the scene. The SAD matcher always uses the grayscale+IR buffer regardless of display mode.
steps 1–2
Hard-set IR stamping — IR dots are now written into the SAD grayscale buffer with a hard set (pixel = 220) rather than additive blend. This ensures each dot creates a sharp, high-contrast anchor regardless of the underlying surface brightness. The block matcher sees a strong, repeatable signal at every dot location — producing far fewer disparity voids on uniform surfaces like the platform.
steps 1–3
SAD stereo matching (full-res) — STEP reduced from 3 to 1 (every pixel), block half-size reduced from 6 to 4 (9×9 block). Smaller blocks respond better to point-feature IR dots without averaging them out. Full-res sampling means no sparse grid gaps for fill to bridge. Combined with hard-set IR, this closes most of the void regions visible in the previous version.
steps 1–2
Stereo vision — Two cameras at a known horizontal offset (baseline). Objects closer to the cameras appear shifted further apart between the two images (high disparity); distant objects shift less. An aperiodic dot pattern is stamped into both camera grayscale buffers before matching runs, simulating an active stereo IR emitter.
step 3
Depth formula Z = f·B/d — Converts disparity to metric depth. f = focal length in pixels, B = baseline in meters, d = disparity in pixels. A disparity of 82px with f=246px and B=1.70m gives Z ≈ 5.1m.
learned matching
U-Net learned stereo — Production systems replace SAD with a trained encoder-decoder network. The training simulation shows convergence: voids fill in, noisy matches sharpen, accuracy asymptotes at ~85% (real network ceiling due to textureless regions and occlusion boundaries).
disparity (GT)
Ground truth disparity — A GPU depth pass from stereoL's exact position gives per-pixel true Z. Disparity is then derived as d = f·B/Z. Auto-ranged so the small depth difference between parts and platform spreads across the full turbo colormap. Compare directly with the computed disparity panel to see where the stereo algorithm loses information.
calibration
Reprojection error — Measures stereo calibration quality: how many pixels a known 3D point projects away from its expected image position. Below 0.5px is good; above 0.5px indicates calibration drift and degrades depth accuracy.
steps 5–6
Inverse kinematics (IK) — Given a target XYZ position for the end effector (suction cup), IK works backward through the arm's joint geometry to compute the joint angles needed to reach it. This sim uses a closed-form analytic solver for a 2-DOF planar arm.
